## MacCrab v1.6.10 ŌĆö Three more FP patterns, closed at the rule level

Overnight soak after v1.6.9 produced three distinct FPs. All three
are fixed at the detection layer rather than via allowlist additions
ŌĆö each had a structural fix that closes a whole family of similar
FPs, not just the specific process that was firing.

### 1. Hidden File Created ŌĆö re-scoped to unsigned/adhoc only

Logitech Options+ agent (`logioptionsplus_agent`) kept firing this
rule despite v1.6.5's vendor allowlist. The compiled rule genuinely
had the Logitech filters ŌĆö verified by inspecting the JSON ŌĆö yet
the alerts kept coming. Either the test daemon was running stale
rules OR the allowlist was brittle against vendor binary renames.

**Fix:** re-scope the rule to require `SignerType in [unsigned, adHoc]`
at the selection stage. Every legitimate vendor peripheral agent
(Logitech, Razer, Elgato, Corsair, SteelSeries, Blackmagic, and
every other HID driver app) is developer-ID signed and never
reaches the selection. The rule now only fires on explicitly-
untrusted binaries ŌĆö which is exactly the threat model it was
written to catch.

This is a stronger model than vendor allowlists: one condition
excludes the entire universe of signed vendor apps instead of
maintaining a perpetually-growing list of product names.

### 2. C2 Beacon Pattern ŌĆö dev-ID-signed /Applications/ apps filtered

"Keynote Creator Studio" (a third-party paid app, NOT Apple's
Keynote) fired the regular-HTTPS-beacon rule 3├Ś in 5 hours ŌĆö
once then 4 hours later then 1 hour after that, so it outran
the 1-hour dedup window each time.

**Fix:** added `filter_devid_applications` to c2_beacon_pattern ŌĆö
drops matches where the process is developer-ID signed AND
installed in `/Applications/`. Covers the long tail of paid
3rd-party apps doing routine licensing / analytics / update
HTTPS. The `/Applications/` anchor keeps the filter narrow:
dropper-staged binaries in `/tmp` or `~/Downloads` still fire
even if they're signed.

Gatekeeper-approved developer-ID apps that turn malicious get
caught by the notarization-revoked and developer-revoked rules,
not by a weak HTTPS-timing heuristic.

### 3. Power Anomaly ŌĆö dasd added to knownLegitimate

`dasd` (Duet Activity Scheduler) is macOS's own background-task
scheduler. Its whole job is to take power assertions long enough
to run scheduled background work. v1.6.0 shipped the
`knownLegitimate` allowlist with 50+ Apple daemons but missed
`dasd` specifically because it rarely appears in the field ŌĆö but
on an overnight-idle machine its `DASActivity:*` assertions are
the dominant signal.

**Fix:** added `dasd` + its companions (`xpcproxy`,
`ScheduleProxy`, `BackgroundTask`, `signpost_reporter_activity`)
plus common always-on cloud sync clients that were missed
(`Dropbox`, `OneDrive`, `Google Drive`, `iCloud Drive`, `Box`,
`Boxclient`, `pboard`, `PowerChime`) to the `knownLegitimate` set.

### Regression harness

Three new unit tests:

- `compiledRuleSignerSelection` ŌĆö guards the hidden_file signer
  anchor against future edits that revert to the v1.6.5 allowlist
  model
- `compiledRuleHasDevIDFilter` ŌĆö verifies c2_beacon's
  filter_devid_applications compiled correctly
- `dasdSuppressed` ŌĆö runs a live `PowerAnomalyDetector.scan()` and
  asserts no anomaly has `processName == "dasd"`

### Structural observation

Each of the three fixes is signer- or daemon-based, not string-
based. Across v1.6.5-v1.6.9 the pattern kept repeating: add a
vendor name ŌåÆ vendor renames binary ŌåÆ FP returns. v1.6.10 flips
every one of those to an anchor the vendor can't rename around
(signer type, install prefix, daemon identity).
